Introduction

The ColorCheckerÂ® Patch Classic target is an array of 24 scientifically prepared natural, chromatic, primary and grayscale colored squares. These calibrated patches cover a broad spectrum of colors. When included in a test image, the ColorChecker serves as a reliable reference for comparing the captured output of cameras to the original chart’s accurate color measurements, ensuring precise color reproduction and consistency.

Color & Chroma Difference

Calculating the color and chroma difference of images with the ColorChecker serves as a quantitative measure of a camera’s color reproduction accuracy.

The CIE Lab color space was specifically designed to model human vision and is widely used for calculating color differences due to the perceptual uniformity: equal distances in the Lab space correspond to equal perceived color differences.

CIE 1976 Standard

The absolute difference of color ÎE* and chroma ÎC* are calculated by measuring the Euclidean distance of two Lab coordinates. Compared to the ÎE formula, the chroma difference ÎC is calculated without considering luminance for better focusing on the color inaccuracies independently of camera exposure error.

CIE 2000 Standard

The formulas of ÎE* and ÎC* are revised to better address the perceptual non-uniformity problem. The CIE 2000 standard introduced three major improvements:

  1. Using new color space LCh model instead of Lab model
  2. Adding a weighting factor to balance lightness, chroma, and hue components
  3. Including a rotational term to account for interactions between chroma and hue

Industrial Implementation

The first step in calculating ÎE* and ÎC* involves converting the RGB color space to the Lab color space, which requires an intermediate transformation to the XYZ color space. As the source and target color spaces may have different reference white points, chromatic adaptation is necessary to ensure an accurate conversion.

In our experiments, we found that Imatest ignores the D50 Chroma Adaptation in its color difference calculation, while iQ-analyzer applies the Bradford matrix for proper chromatic adaptation.

This difference in implementation leads to variations in the calculated color difference values, which is crucial for understanding when comparing results across different analysis platforms.

Methodology

Color Space Conversion

The conversion from RGB to Lab color space requires an intermediate transformation through XYZ color space. The process involves:

  1. RGB to XYZ: Using the standard RGB2XYZ transformation matrix (M1)
  2. Chromatic Adaptation: Applying Bradford matrix (M2) for D50 adaptation when required
  3. XYZ to Lab: Final conversion to perceptually uniform Lab space

Color Difference Metrics

Two main standards for calculating color differences:

  • CIE 1976 (ÎE*ab): Simple Euclidean distance in Lab space
  • CIE 2000 (ÎE*00): Advanced formula with weighting factors and rotational terms
